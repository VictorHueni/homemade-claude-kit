# Business Vision — Methodology References

Canonical bibliography for the `business-vision` skill. **Lives only in the kit** — never copied to projects. Project vision docs link to this file via the 2-line methodology pointer in their header.

---

## Primary sources

### Positioning format

Moore, G.A. (1991; updated 2014). *Crossing the Chasm: Marketing and Selling High-Tech Products to Mainstream Customers*, 3rd ed. HarperBusiness.
Last verified: 2026-05-21

The "elevator pitch" / positioning statement format used in §The Elevator Pitch:
> "For [target customer] who [statement of need or opportunity], the [product name] is a [product category] that [statement of key benefit]. Unlike [primary competitive alternative], our product [statement of primary differentiation]."

The format works because it forces six specific answers: (a) audience, (b) problem, (c) product category, (d) benefit, (e) specific alternative, (f) differentiation. Vagueness in any slot reveals strategic confusion.

### WHY anchoring

Sinek, S. (2009). *Start With Why: How Great Leaders Inspire Everyone to Take Action.* Portfolio/Penguin.
Last verified: 2026-05-21

The Golden Circle: WHY (purpose) → HOW (process) → WHAT (result). The vision anchors the WHY layer — the reason the product exists, independent of what it does or how. Most product documents start with WHAT (features) and work backward to justification. This skill reverses that: start with WHY, derive WHAT from it.

### Product vision structure

Pichler, R. (2016). *Strategize: Product Strategy and Product Roadmap Practices for the Digital Age.* Pichler Consulting.
Last verified: 2026-05-21

The Product Vision Board: (1) Vision statement, (2) Target group, (3) Needs, (4) Product description, (5) Business goals. The five sections of `business-vision`'s template map directly to this structure. Pichler's key discipline: the vision must be motivating and inspiring — developers should be able to read it and understand WHY their daily work matters.

### Product vision as north star

Cagan, M. (2017). *Inspired: How to Create Tech Products Customers Love*, 2nd ed. Wiley.
Last verified: 2026-05-21

Product vision as the north star for the product team: inspiring, customer-centric, timeless (3–10 year horizon), not a product roadmap. Cagan's key principle: the product vision should not describe the product you're building next quarter — it should describe the product you're trying to create over the next several years.

### North Star Metric

Ellis, S. & Brown, M. (2017). *Hacking Growth: How Today's Fastest-Growing Companies Drive Breakout Success.* Currency.
Last verified: 2026-05-21

The North Star Metric: the single metric that best captures the core value the product delivers to customers. Properties of a good North Star: (1) reflects value delivered to the user (not just business revenue), (2) is a leading indicator of long-term success, (3) is measurable in the current product, (4) is directional (more/less is unambiguously better). Distinct from quarterly OKR targets — the North Star is the direction; KRs are the distance covered toward it this quarter.

## Further reading

Olsen, D. (2015). *The Lean Product Playbook.* Wiley. — Product-market fit pyramid; the vision sits at the "underserved needs" and "value proposition" layers.

Gothelf, J. & Seiden, J. (2016). *Lean UX*, 2nd ed. O'Reilly. — Assumption-driven product vision; the vision as a statement of hypotheses to test.

Wodtke, C. (2021). *The Team That Managed Itself.* Cucina Media. — Vision as the alignment tool for autonomous teams; complement to Doerr's OKR framework for the `business-objective` skill.
