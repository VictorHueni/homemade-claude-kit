# Business Objective — Methodology References

Canonical bibliography for the `business-objective` skill. **Lives only in the kit** — never copied to projects. Project objective docs link to this file via the 2-line methodology pointer in their header.

---

## Primary sources

### OKR structure

Doerr, J. (2018). *Measure What Matters: OKRs and the Science of Focusing Your Entire Organization on What Really Matters.* Portfolio/Penguin.
Last verified: 2026-05-21

The canonical OKR reference. Doerr synthesises Andy Grove's Intel OKR practice with Google's organisational application. Key principles used in this skill:
- Objectives are qualitative and inspiring; Key Results are measurable outcomes.
- A Key Result is not a task or feature delivery — it is a metric-change statement.
- 3–5 Key Results per objective; 2–5 objectives per cycle.
- OKRs are public, graded, and reviewed on a cadence (quarterly or annual).
- 0.6–0.7 average score on an ambitious KR is healthy; consistent 1.0 means targets are too low.

### Business requirements vocabulary

BABOK v3 §1.3. IIBA (2015). *A Guide to the Business Analysis Body of Knowledge*, 3rd ed. International Institute of Business Analysis.
Last verified: 2026-05-21

Vocabulary anchor: "Business Requirements describe the goals, objectives, and outcomes that the enterprise needs to achieve. They may include the purpose of an initiative, the things the business needs to be able to do to meet the purpose, and the metrics that will be used to measure success." Used to maintain consistency with the kit's existing BABOK citations in `spec-quality-attributes`, `spec-functional-breakdown-structure`, and `spec-delivery-roadmap`.

### Outcome discipline

Adzic, G. (2012). *Impact Mapping: Making a Big Impact with Software Products and Projects.* Provoking Thoughts.
Last verified: 2026-05-21

The WHY → WHO → HOW → WHAT cascade. Impact Mapping's core discipline: deliverables (WHAT) only make sense when traced to impacts (HOW — behaviour changes), which only make sense when traced to actors (WHO), which only make sense when traced to goals (WHY). This skill borrows the outcome discipline from Impact Mapping: Key Results must be *impact statements* (behaviour or metric changes), never deliverable statements (features or story points).

### Multi-dimensional classification

Kaplan, R.S. & Norton, D.P. (1992). "The Balanced Scorecard — Measures that Drive Performance." *Harvard Business Review*, 70(1), 71–79.
Last verified: 2026-05-21

The 4-perspective model (Financial / Customer / Internal Process / Learning & Growth) is used as a **tag only** in this skill — not as the primary structure. Adding the BSC perspective tag to each OBJ-NN ensures the objectives set is balanced across stakeholder dimensions without requiring the full BSC overhead (strategy maps, balanced scorecards per business unit).

## Further reading

Grove, A. (1983). *High Output Management.* Random House. — Original source of OKRs at Intel; the management context behind Doerr's framework.

Wodtke, C. (2016). *Radical Focus: Achieving Your Most Important Goals with Objectives and Key Results.* Boxes and Arrows. — Practical OKR guide for small teams and startups; useful for sizing and cadence calibration.

Gothelf, J. & Seiden, J. (2021). *Outcomes Over Output: Why Customer Behavior Is the Key Metric for Business Success.* Sense & Respond Press. — Reinforces the outcome discipline; direct complement to Adzic's Impact Mapping.

Cagan, M. (2017). *Inspired: How to Create Tech Products Customers Love*, 2nd ed. Wiley. — Product objectives in the context of product discovery; OKR alignment with product strategy.
